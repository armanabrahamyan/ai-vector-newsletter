Trust is not static. Across compliance, coding, evaluation, and agent behaviour, today's research shares one finding: AI systems drift or distort over time. Governance built on point-in-time checks will not hold.

A runtime compliance score could replace the once-a-year AI audit

Treat-it-once audit logic doesn't satisfy the EU AI Act's continuous-oversight requirement. govllm routes model selection by live compliance scores, using a panel of small on-premise language models as regulatory judges. On a 49-pair test corpus, agreement topped out at 69%. No independent benchmark exists yet. Raise this architecture at your next model-risk review before committing to a static audit approach.

AI coding agents quietly change personality during long sessions, new benchmark shows

AI coding agents drift from their trained persona across long sessions. ContextEcho tests 23 models on sessions up to 9,716 turns and finds drift is universal across model families. A single re-anchor prompt restores behaviour. Academic paper only, no production tooling yet; run the open-source harness against your agent before shipping.

Conversation history quietly skews AI judgements, even across top models

When a language model evaluates items in sequence, prior positive or negative results contaminate subsequent scores. Across nearly 76,000 API calls spanning 11 models, negative histories produced 1.6 times more bias than positive ones. Larger models reduced but didn't eliminate the effect. The fix is simple: use a fresh context per evaluation item before trusting any batched scoring pipeline.

Multi-agent AI systems reach false consensus in nearly all cases

Scoring final answers misses how multi-agent systems actually fail. MedAgentAudit, auditing 14,400 cases across six architectures, found agents repeat initial views without re-examining evidence in 98% of discussions, and unsupported observations propagate in 17% of cases. No code is released yet; raise the taxonomy of ten failure modes at your next agentic system design review.
